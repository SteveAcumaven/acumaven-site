Most companies shopping for an Acumatica partner ask the same questions: How many implementations have you done? How long have you been an Acumatica partner? Can you send us some references?

These questions are not useless. But they're also easy to answer well without revealing anything meaningful. A firm can have done 200 implementations — most of them mediocre. A five-year Acumatica partner can still staff your project with someone who certified three months ago. References are handpicked.

I've been on both sides of this evaluation. I've won projects and I've lost projects — including some I lost to cheaper firms who called me six months later to ask if I could rescue what they'd built. So I have a pretty clear view of which questions actually separate good partners from expensive mistakes.

Here are the ones worth asking.

---

## "Can you walk me through a project that went over budget — and whose fault was it?"

This is the question that tells you the most about a consultant's character and self-awareness.

Every experienced consultant has a project that went over budget or sideways in some way. ERP implementations are complex, clients' businesses are complex, and something always surprises you. What matters is how the consultant handles it when things go wrong.

**What a good answer sounds like:** They describe a specific project, acknowledge what they underestimated or could have handled better, explain what they changed in their process afterward, and — if client factors contributed — describe them honestly without being defensive or blame-shifting. "We underestimated the complexity of their multi-entity setup, and we absorbed some of the overrun because we didn't scope it properly."

**Red flag:** Any version of "all our projects come in on time and budget." That's not true, and the fact that they're saying it means they're either delusional or they're comfortable telling you things that aren't true. Neither is a quality you want in someone who will be embedded in your business for months.

---

## "Who specifically will be doing the work on our project?"

Not "what does your team look like" — *who specifically*. Get names.

The consulting industry has a standard move: the senior, impressive person does the sales process, then the work gets handed off to a junior analyst you've never met. This isn't necessarily malicious — it's just how consulting firms work. But if that's happening, you should know about it before you sign, not after.

**What to do with the answer:** Ask to meet the people who will actually be on your project before you sign the contract. Ask about their experience with companies your size and in your industry. Ask how many other projects they're currently staffed on. A consultant running four concurrent implementations is not giving any of them full attention.

**Red flag:** Vague answers about "our team" and "our resources," or reluctance to introduce you to the actual analyst before the contract is signed.

---

## "What are the top three reasons your past implementations have struggled?"

A version of the first question, but broader — and the framing matters. You're not asking them to confess to failure; you're asking them to demonstrate self-awareness about the patterns they've observed.

**What a good answer sounds like:** A thoughtful list that includes both client-side and consultant-side factors. Something like: "Clients who don't have an internal champion who can make decisions, scope that wasn't clearly defined before kickoff, and data migration that got underestimated." This answer is honest and also teaches you something about what you need to do to make the project succeed.

**Red flag:** "Our implementations don't really struggle" or a list that's entirely about client behavior — unresponsive clients, clients who kept changing requirements, clients who didn't train their users. A consultant who puts all struggle on the client side has a blind spot that will show up on your project.

---

## "Have you implemented Acumatica for a company in our specific industry?"

Generic Acumatica experience is not the same as field service experience or distribution experience. The platform is broad, and the configuration for a field service company — scheduling, dispatching, work orders, mobile technician apps, service billing — is fundamentally different from the configuration for a distribution company or a professional services firm.

Industry experience means your consultant knows the common edge cases before they bite you. They know where Acumatica's field service module is strong and where it needs workarounds. They know what a field service company's chart of accounts typically looks like, how job costing flows, how to handle warranty claims.

**What to do with the answer:** If they have field service experience, ask specifically which modules they've implemented — Field Service, Project Accounting, Service and Support. Ask about complexity: multi-location? Union labor? Equipment tracking? The more specific they can get, the more confident you can be that the experience is real.

**Red flag:** "We've done a lot of service companies" or pivoting to general Acumatica expertise. General expertise is table stakes. Industry experience is the differentiator.

---

## "What does your go-live week actually look like?"

This question tests whether they have a real process or whether go-live is just a date on a project plan.

A consultant with a mature implementation process will describe something specific: a structured cutover checklist, parallel run period, data migration validation steps, a hypercare period immediately post-go-live with daily check-ins, defined rollback criteria if something goes catastrophically wrong. They've done this enough times that they have a playbook.

**What a good answer sounds like:** "We do a cutover weekend — Thursday we freeze transactions in the old system, run final data migration, validate against our checklist Friday. Monday morning we're there on-site for the first week, running daily standups, triaging issues in real time. We don't disappear after go-live."

**Red flag:** Generic language about "transition" and "support" without specifics. Or a go-live plan that amounts to "we flip the switch and you call us if something breaks." If they don't have a structured hypercare process, your first weeks in the new system are going to be chaotic.

---

## "Do you do fixed-price or time-and-materials — and why?"

Neither model is inherently right or wrong. But how a consultant answers this question reveals how they think about risk and who they expect to absorb it.

Fixed-price contracts protect the client from scope creep but require extremely precise requirements upfront. If the requirements aren't nailed down — and they rarely are at the start of an ERP project — a fixed-price contract either means the consultant padded the estimate significantly to cover unknowns, or they'll nickel-and-dime you with change orders for anything that wasn't spelled out explicitly.

T&M gives you flexibility but puts the cost risk on you. It's appropriate when scope is genuinely uncertain, but it requires active management and clear communication about hours.

**What a good answer sounds like:** An honest explanation of the tradeoffs, and a proposal for which model fits your specific situation and why. Not a canned defense of whatever model that firm prefers.

**Red flag:** A fixed-price bid that comes in significantly lower than other proposals. Someone is either very efficient or very optimistic. In ERP implementations, optimism is expensive.

---

## "What's your process when we discover mid-project that a requirement was wrong?"

Requirements change. Businesses are more complex than anyone realizes at the start of a project, and something always surfaces mid-implementation that wasn't anticipated. What matters is how the consultant handles it.

**What a good answer sounds like:** A clear process — formal change control, documented change requests, honest conversation about the cost and timeline implications of the change, and a decision gate where the client decides whether to proceed. They treat the discovery of wrong requirements as a normal part of the process, not a failure.

**Red flag:** "We just handle it" or defensiveness about the question. A consultant without a change management process will either absorb scope changes silently (and burn out or cut corners) or handle them informally in ways that create disputes about money and timeline later.

---

## The Pattern in These Questions

What you're actually evaluating across all of these is: does this consultant have a mature, honest process — and are they willing to be transparent about it?

The best Acumatica partners have been through enough implementations to know where projects fail, and they've built their process around preventing those failures. They can talk specifically about what they do and why. They don't hide the junior analyst. They don't pretend projects never go sideways.

The firms to avoid are the ones who have a polished answer for every question that makes everything sound easy and risk-free. Nothing about an ERP implementation is easy and risk-free. The question is whether your consultant has the experience and honesty to navigate the hard parts with you.
